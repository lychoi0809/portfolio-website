Personal Essay 🏆 Rachel Carson Intergenerational Sense of Wonder / Sense of the Wild Writing Contest — 1st Place

Where Life Endures: Discoveries in the DMZ

A walk through the Korean Demilitarized Zone — where the rusted remains of land mines share the ground with leopard cat footprints, egrets, and the first shoots of spring. An essay about what it means to find life flourishing in the most unlikely of places.

"Something clanks against my shoe as I walk. I look down to see the rusted remains of a land mine. It is a remnant of war, a vestige of historical suffering. Next to it, a white lily peeks shyly out of the ground, crossing over the metal. The contrast strikes me: an instrument of death, woven through with a lovely little touch of life."

Academic Essay · International Relations 🏆 Harvard International Review Academic Writing Contest — Gold Medal

Lithium as Leverage: Bolivia's Role in the 21st-Century Power Struggle

As the world races toward decarbonization, lithium has become the new oil — and Bolivia, home to the world's largest reserves, finds itself at the center of a geopolitical scramble between China, Russia, the United States, and the EU. This essay argues that Bolivia is not merely a passive prize in this contest, but an active player using competing powers' hunger for its resources as leverage to dictate the terms of extraction, processing, and profit — and potentially redefine what it means to be a small state in a resource-hungry world.

Academic Essay · Linguistics ✦ Minds Underground Essay Competition — Highly Commended

What Is Lost When Translating Literature from One Language to Another?

Beginning with the experience of reading Yoon Dong-ju's poems first in Korean and then in English — and the quiet disappointment of the second encounter — this essay examines what makes literary translation so fundamentally incomplete. Drawing on Korean, Japanese, Chinese, and French literature, it traces how grammar, structure, honorifics, and cultural implication create meaning that cannot survive intact across languages. In the end, the question is not whether something is lost, but what a translator chooses to sacrifice: fidelity to the original words, or fidelity to the original feeling.
