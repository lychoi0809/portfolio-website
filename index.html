Bilingual Evaluation of Tone and Emotional Nuance in Korean–English Translation: Human vs. AI

Investigated how Korean–English bilinguals evaluate translation quality across human and AI outputs. Conducted Likert-style ratings and open-ended responses with 20 participants evaluating a Korean folktale. Found that participants prioritized emotional authenticity over literal accuracy, with only 60% correctly identifying AI involvement, suggesting translation quality depends on how each approach handles specific cultural challenges, not whether it was human or machine-produced.

02
linguistics · corpus · historical

Frequency Accelerates Semantic Change: Evidence from 500 Years of Korean

Challenged the "law of conformity," the long-held assumption that high-frequency words resist semantic change. Trained diachronic word embeddings on historical Korean corpora spanning the 15th to 20th centuries and found the opposite: high-frequency words changed more, not less. Validated findings across six robustness controls and an English replication, linking the reversal to grammaticalization and semantic bleaching processes unique to Korean typology.

Characteristics and Species Diversity of S. angulatus Colonies in the Imjin River Basin of the Western Civilian Control Line

Conducted field research in the western civilian control line to analyze the spread and ecological impact of bur cucumber (Sicyos angulatus), an invasive species threatening native riparian vegetation across South Korea. Surveyed three habitat types along the Imjin River and found the species concentrated in riverbank and forest-edge environments. Results contribute to conservation understanding of one of the world's most ecologically unique and understudied landscapes.

Igbo Language Textbook

a textbook written from scratch to support igbo heritage classes in korea, for children reconnecting with the language their families carry from home.

[ add photos of your drafts, the community, or textbook pages here ]

In a Nigerian community in Korea, many parents speak Igbo and English fluently while their children, raised here and educated in Korean schools, have grown up with Korean as one of their strongest languages. Some speak little or no Igbo. Community members recently started Igbo classes to address this, but beginner-level teaching materials simply don't exist for this context.

I'm developing those materials. Having spent time with the children and families, I'm building something that meets them where they are: designed around the languages they already know, the culture they're reconnecting with, and the reality of learning a heritage language as a child growing up between worlds.

Moonlight Children's Choir

a choir for refugee and migrant children in korea, which i founded and direct, built on the belief that music is one of the most honest languages we share.

[ add photos of rehearsals or performances here ]

Children who have moved across borders, who carry more than one language and more than one sense of home, deserve a space where all of that is welcome. I founded Moonlight Children's Choir to be that space: a place to be creative, expressive, and fully yourself, finding hope and song and laughter together even when things are rough.

The name was chosen together with the children. Moonlight because the choir is meant to shine in the darkness. I direct, arrange, and sometimes write for the ensemble, which rehearses regularly and performs publicly.

Lithium as Leverage: Bolivia's Role in the 21st-Century Power Struggle

Analyzed how Bolivia, one of Latin America's poorest countries, has leveraged its position as home to the world's largest lithium reserves to gain disproportionate geopolitical influence amid a global scramble for battery materials. Argued that Bolivia is not a passive prize in great power competition but an active player dictating extraction terms and potentially a model for other resource-rich emerging states.

academic essay · linguistics
✦ minds underground essay competition — highly commended

What Is Lost When Translating Literature from One Language to Another?

Starting from the experience of reading Yoon Dong-ju's poems first in Korean and then in English, and the quiet disappointment of the second encounter, examined how grammar, honorifics, structural logic, and cultural implication create meaning that cannot survive intact across languages. Drew on Korean, Japanese, Chinese, and French literature to argue that translators must always choose between fidelity to words or fidelity to feeling.
